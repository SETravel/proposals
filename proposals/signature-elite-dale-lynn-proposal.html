Stage One

Wichita Falls to Seattle

Dale's preferred route: US 287 to Denver, then I-25 → I-80 → US 30 → I-86 → I-84 → US 20 → I-5. Roughly 2,300 miles. We've broken it into 7 driving days plus the Eugene stop.
A note before we start: this route is your route — the one Dale laid out. We've worked with it, not around it. The first three days are honest plains driving (the price of admission for the rest of the country); from Denver west, the scenery starts paying you back. By the time you hit the Snake River Plain in Idaho you're already in the West proper. Plan to leave Wichita Falls around Tuesday, May 26. That puts you in Eugene the evening of June 1 and Seattle by mid-afternoon June 2 — a full day to settle in before sailing.
Day 1 · Tuesday, May 26
Wichita Falls → Lamar, Colorado
~430 mi · ~7 hr

US 287 north straight up through Vernon, Childress, Amarillo, Dumas. The Texas panhandle gives way to the Oklahoma panhandle gives way to southeast Colorado. By dinnertime you're in Lamar, CO — a little ranching town where US 287 meets US 50. Nothing fancy here, just an easy stop for the night.

Worth pulling off for: The Cadillac Ranch west of Amarillo (free, 10 minutes off the highway, you've probably seen it on TV) and The Big Texan Steak Ranch if it's lunchtime — same place with the 72-oz steak challenge, also fine for a regular meal.

Suggested Overnight
Holiday Inn Express Lamar or Best Western Cow Palace Inn — both reliable, both have free parking and easy in-and-out.
Day 2 · Wednesday, May 27
Lamar → Denver, CO
~210 mi · ~3.5 hr

A short driving day on purpose. US 287 north to Kit Carson, then US 40 / I-70 west into Denver. The Rockies appear ahead of you on the horizon for hours before you actually reach them — one of those views that only happens in the West.

With a half-day in Denver, you have time for an early dinner downtown and a sunset look at the city. If you've never seen them: Red Rocks Amphitheatre is 20 minutes west of town — you can drive right up, walk into the amphitheater for free, and the view back over Denver at sunset is one of the best in Colorado.

Suggested Overnight
We'd point you toward a Denver Tech Center or West Denver hotel (Hampton Inn, Hyatt Place) — easier parking and a clean exit to I-25 north in the morning.
Day 3 · Thursday, May 28
Denver → Rock Springs, WY
~330 mi · ~5 hr

I-25 up to Cheyenne, then I-80 west across southern Wyoming. This is the high plains — wide, empty, beautiful in its own austere way. You'll pass through Laramie and Rawlins, with the Snowy Range visible to the south for much of the morning.

Quick stop: The Lincoln Highway Monument at Sherman Summit between Cheyenne and Laramie — just a pull-off, but it's the highest point on the original cross-country highway and there's a fine bust of Lincoln watching the trucks go by.

Suggested Overnight
Holiday Inn Rock Springs or Hampton Inn Rock Springs — both right off I-80, both quiet.
Day 4 · Friday, May 29
Rock Springs → Twin Falls, ID
~340 mi · ~5 hr

I-80 west to the Utah border, then a quick swing through the corner of Utah onto US 30 / I-84 northwest into Idaho. You'll be in the Snake River Plain by lunch — cottonwoods along the river, lava fields, irrigation green.

If you have an hour to spare: Shoshone Falls in Twin Falls is called the "Niagara of the West" and in late May / early June it's running at peak from the spring snowmelt. Five minutes off I-84, $5 to park, and the falls are right there. Don't skip this one — the timing is perfect for your trip.

Suggested Overnight
Hampton Inn Twin Falls or Hilton Garden Inn Twin Falls — both walking distance to dinner and an easy launch in the morning.
Day 5 · Saturday, May 30
Twin Falls → Boise → Baker City, OR
~290 mi · ~4.5 hr

I-84 west across the rest of Idaho. You'll pass through Boise around midday — a good spot to stop for lunch if you want to stretch the legs in a real downtown. From there, I-84 climbs up into eastern Oregon and the landscape starts to change — first sagebrush, then the foothills of the Blue Mountains.

Baker City is a small, well-preserved historic town in the Powder River Valley — the kind of place where the main street still looks like it did in 1900. If it's open when you arrive: the National Historic Oregon Trail Interpretive Center just outside town is one of the best museums on the entire trip. Real wagon ruts visible from the trail.

Suggested Overnight
Best Western Sunridge Inn Baker City — quiet, easy parking, walkable to a couple of decent restaurants downtown.
Day 6 · Sunday, May 31
Baker City → Bend, OR
~270 mi · ~4.5 hr

I-84 to La Grande, then south on US 26 / US 20 through the John Day country — one of the most beautiful stretches you'll drive on this whole trip and most people never see it. Sage hills, painted rock formations, ranches that haven't changed in 80 years.

The reason for this routing: it puts you into Bend instead of Portland, which means an easier drive into Eugene tomorrow and far less Portland traffic. Bend itself is a fun little mountain town — if you arrive in time, drive up to Pilot Butte in town for a 360° view of the Cascades. You can see Mt. Bachelor, the Three Sisters, and Mt. Hood from one spot.

Routing note
If Dale prefers to stay on his original I-84 → I-5 routing through Portland, that's a longer day (~410 mi). Our suggestion of going via Bend is mostly for your comfort — less interstate, lighter traffic, prettier driving. Either works.
Suggested Overnight
Hampton Inn Bend or Oxford Hotel Bend — the Oxford is a small step up if you want to treat yourselves at the halfway mark.
Day 7 · Monday, June 1
Bend → Eugene, OR
~130 mi · ~2.5 hr

A short, easy day on purpose — this is your granddaughter day. US 20 west over McKenzie Pass into the Willamette Valley. The pass tops out at ~5,300 ft and the views back toward the Three Sisters are something to remember; if there's still snow on the pass (sometimes there is in early June), the alternate Santiam Pass on US 20 stays open year-round.

Arrive Eugene early afternoon. You'll have all evening for dinner with your granddaughter. Stay overnight here — pushing on to Seattle the same day is doable but it puts you on the road until late and you'd lose the dinner. One night in Eugene and you arrive Seattle relaxed.

Suggested Overnight
Hilton Eugene or Hyatt Place Eugene/Oakway — both walkable to a number of restaurants. Pick whichever is closer to where you're meeting your granddaughter.
Day 8 · Tuesday, June 2
Eugene → Seattle, WA
~290 mi · ~4.5 hr

I-5 north all the way. You'll pass Portland mid-morning (try to clear it before 11 if you can — traffic builds after that), cross the Columbia River into Washington, and arrive Seattle by mid-afternoon. That gives you all of June 2 evening and all of June 3 in the city before sailing on June 4.

Hotel options for those two pre-cruise nights are in the next section.

Next: A Few Notes on the Cruise
June 4 → June 11

The Inside Passage, this time around

Since you've sailed this route before, the cruise needs no introduction. A few small things are easy to miss the first time — here are the ones we'd flag.
Your itinerary on the Carnival Miracle is the classic Seattle round-trip: Tracy Arm Fjord cruising → Skagway → Juneau → Ketchikan → Victoria, BC → back to Seattle. One sea day each way. You know the ports. So instead of an itinerary, here are five small things experienced second-time cruisers tell us they wish they'd done the first time.
1. The Tracy Arm dawn

The ship enters Tracy Arm Fjord very early — usually before 6 a.m. Most passengers are asleep. Set an alarm. Bring a thermos of coffee from the Lido buffet (it opens at 5) up to the top deck on the port (left) side of the ship. The way the granite walls close in around you, the sound of glacier ice cracking, the seals on icebergs — that hour is the single best hour of the entire cruise and it's wasted if you sleep through it. Layer up; it's cold.

2. Skagway: walk past the cruise pier

Most cruise passengers stay in the first two blocks of Broadway — the gift shops near the dock. Walk eight more blocks east and you're in residential Skagway, where the locals live. The Gold Rush Cemetery is a 20-minute walk and tells the real story (Soapy Smith, the con-artist gangster, is buried there). It's free and most people miss it entirely.

3. Juneau: the Mendenhall back trail

If you took the standard Mendenhall Glacier visit last time, you saw it from the visitor center side. This time — if your knees allow it — ask a cab driver to drop you at the Mendenhall Glacier Trail of Time (the Photo Point trail). It's a flat, paved half-mile that ends at a beach where you can see the glacier from a completely different angle, with Nugget Falls thundering next to it. Most cruisers never see it from this side.

4. Ketchikan: Creek Street at low tide

Creek Street is the famous boardwalk you've already seen. What changes by the hour: the tide. At low tide, you can walk down the steps to the creek itself and watch salmon trying to jump up the falls (in early June it's just starting). Worth checking the tide tables when you wake up that morning — the difference between high and low tide here is dramatic.

5. Victoria, BC: stay onboard for the sailaway

Victoria is the last port and the ship is in late afternoon to evening. Most passengers go ashore for Butchart Gardens. If you've done that already, here's the contrarian move: skip going ashore, have a quiet dinner on the ship while it's empty, and watch the sailaway from the top deck around 11 p.m. The harbor lights, the Empress Hotel lit up, the slow turn out of the channel — one of the most peaceful evenings you'll have on the whole cruise.

Stage Two · The Long Way Home

Seattle to Amery, playing the hits

This is the trip Dale wanted. The route home runs through Yellowstone, Grand Teton, the Beartooth and Cloud Peak country, Cody, Devils Tower, Sturgis, Mt. Rushmore, and the Badlands. We've spread it over 12 days — no day pushes 500 miles, several are short on purpose so you can actually see the place you're standing in.
A note on the routing: Dale said "I-90 to Wisconsin with the cool stuff in between," and we built it that way — with two adjustments worth flagging. First: we've added two days for Grand Teton and Jackson. The Tetons are 90 minutes south of Yellowstone and not seeing them when you're already this close would be a real loss. Second: the geography wants you to go Yellowstone → Tetons → Togwotee Pass → Cody → Cloud Peak → Devils Tower → Sturgis → Rushmore → Badlands → home. That's the playing-the-hits sequence with no backtracking. We've laid it out that way.
Day 1 · Friday, June 12
Seattle → Spokane, WA
~280 mi · ~4.5 hr

You'll get off the ship the morning of the 11th. Spend that night in Seattle — you'll be tired, traffic east is bad in the afternoon, and a real night's sleep before driving makes a difference. Hit the road June 12.

I-90 east straight to Spokane. The drive is mostly Cascades coming down into the dry plateau of eastern Washington — pretty but not stop-the-car pretty. Spokane is a real city with good dinner options.

Suggested Overnight
Davenport Grand or Hotel Indigo Spokane — the historic Davenport (a step up) is one of the legendary railroad-era hotels of the West and worth a look if you want a treat.
Day 2 · Saturday, June 13
Spokane → Bozeman, MT
~410 mi · ~6 hr

The longest day on the way home, on purpose — this gets you in position for Yellowstone. I-90 across the panhandle of Idaho (Coeur d'Alene is gorgeous if you have time for a coffee stop) and into Montana proper. Lookout Pass at the state line is the high point.

Bozeman is a fun, affluent college-and-Yellowstone town. The Museum of the Rockies here has one of the best dinosaur fossil collections in the world if it's a rainy afternoon — otherwise, save your energy.

Suggested Overnight
Hilton Garden Inn Bozeman or Element Bozeman — both clean, quiet, easy parking.
Day 3 · Sunday, June 14
Bozeman → Mammoth Hot Springs (Yellowstone)
~80 mi · ~2 hr

A very short driving day, on purpose. US 89 south through the Paradise Valley (the cottonwoods along the Yellowstone River, the Absaroka Range to the east — Robert Redford filmed A River Runs Through It here) and into the park's North Entrance at Gardiner.

You'll arrive Mammoth Hot Springs by lunch with the entire afternoon to walk the boardwalks — the travertine terraces are otherworldly. Elk graze on the lawn at Mammoth in summer; you'll be 15 feet from them. Don't miss the Upper Terrace drive — cars only, very few people, much quieter than the lower boardwalks.

Suggested Overnight · Book WAY in advance
Mammoth Hot Springs Hotel — inside the park, books up 6+ months out. If full, fall back to Yellowstone Hot Springs Lodge in Gardiner just outside the gate.
Important · Park lodging
Yellowstone in-park lodging is reserved months in advance through xanterra.com. Tell us today if you want the in-park stay and we'll start working on it immediately. Outside-the-park hotels (Gardiner / West Yellowstone) are easier to get last-minute.
Day 4 · Monday, June 15
Mammoth → Norris → Old Faithful
~70 mi · ~5 hr w/ stops

This is your full Yellowstone playing-the-hits day, and we're keeping it short on miles so you can actually walk and look. South from Mammoth to Norris Geyser Basin first — raw, hissing, sulfurous, the most thermally active spot on the entire planet. Then continue south through Madison (along the river, watch for elk and bison) to the geyser basins around Old Faithful.

Grand Prismatic Spring is between Madison and Old Faithful. Most people pull into the parking lot, walk the boardwalk over the spring, and miss the best view entirely. The trick: drive a half-mile south to the Fairy Falls trailhead and walk the flat 0.6-mile trail to the Grand Prismatic overlook — the bird's-eye view that's on every Yellowstone postcard. It's the difference between standing next to it and seeing it.

Arrive Old Faithful in the afternoon. Watch one eruption (predicted every ~90 minutes; the visitor center posts the next time). Then settle in.

Suggested Overnight · The big one
Old Faithful Inn — the largest log structure in the world, built in 1903, an architectural wonder. Sit in the lobby fireplace with a drink before dinner; the geyser is a 90-second walk out the front door, and you can watch it erupt at midnight from the upper deck with no one around. This is the night to splurge if you splurge anywhere on the road home.
Important · Park lodging
Old Faithful Inn books up fastest of any in-park property — often 8–12 months ahead for mid-June. Tell us today if you want it and we'll start working on it tonight. Backup if full: Snow Lodge across the parking lot (modern, quieter), or stay outside the park in West Yellowstone.
Day 5 · Tuesday, June 16
Old Faithful → Yellowstone Lake → Grand Teton
~115 mi · ~4 hr w/ stops

Out of the geyser country, east to Yellowstone Lake. Stop at the Lake Yellowstone Hotel for a coffee on the porch — built 1891, all yellow clapboard and white columns, it looks like it was placed there by a movie set designer. Walk the lakeshore path for ten minutes; the lake is enormous (87,000 acres at 7,700 ft elevation) and almost always has loons and trumpeter swans on it.

Continue south out the South Entrance on US 89/191. This stretch is one of the best transitions on the entire trip: you leave the volcanic plateau of Yellowstone, drop down through forest, and the first time the Teton Range comes into view across Jackson Lake, you'll understand why Ansel Adams couldn't stop photographing it.

Drive the Inner Park Road through Grand Teton (Teton Park Road, parallel to and west of the main highway) — it puts you right at the base of the mountains. Stops worth making: Jenny Lake (the iconic photo spot), the Chapel of the Transfiguration (a tiny log chapel framing the Tetons through its altar window — one of the most photographed small buildings in America), and the Mormon Row barns if the light is good.

Suggested Overnight
Jackson Lake Lodge (in-park, panoramic Teton view from the lobby's 60-foot windows — books up early) or Cowboy Village Resort in Jackson (cabin-style, reasonable rates, 2 blocks from Town Square). The Lodge is the special-occasion choice; Cowboy Village is the practical choice.
Day 6 · Wednesday, June 17
Grand Teton + Jackson
~50 mi loop

A full day with no long drive — the kind of day this trip needs in the middle. Sleep in. Breakfast somewhere with a Teton view (Jackson Lake Lodge dining room if you stayed there; otherwise The Bunnery or Persephone in Jackson Town Square).

The morning is for the mountains. Two easy options depending on energy: (1) the Jenny Lake shuttle boat — takes you across the lake in 12 minutes, drops you at a flat trail that loops past Hidden Falls; total time about 90 minutes, no real climbing required. Or (2) the scenic drive only — do the full Inner Park Road loop, stopping at every turnout. Schwabacher Landing for the river-and-Tetons reflection photo, the Snake River Overlook (where Ansel Adams took that photograph), Oxbow Bend for moose at dusk.

Afternoon in Jackson itself. The Town Square has the famous antler arches at all four corners. Don't miss: the National Museum of Wildlife Art on the north edge of town (a beautiful, surprising museum with Bierstadt and Russell paintings); the Million Dollar Cowboy Bar if just to look (saddle-stools at the bar); and dinner at The Gun Barrel Steakhouse or Snake River Brewing — both casual, both good, neither requires a reservation a month out.

Suggested Overnight
Same hotel as last night — saves you packing the car.
Day 7 · Thursday, June 18
Jackson → Togwotee Pass → Dubois → Cody
~180 mi · ~4 hr w/ stops

Out of Jackson north on US 26/287, retracing yesterday morning's drive briefly, then turning east at Moran Junction toward Togwotee Pass (pronounced TOH-guh-tee). This is one of the great unsung scenic drives in the West — the climb out of Jackson Hole gives you the last, biggest, longest view of the Teton Range receding behind you in your rearview mirror.

The pass tops out at 9,655 ft on the Continental Divide. Stop at the Togwotee Overlook just past Togwotee Mountain Lodge — a paved pull-off with what's been called the greatest single highway view of the Tetons that exists. Take the picture there.

Down the eastern slope into Dubois, a sleepy frontier town in the Wind River Valley (population ~900, no stoplight, surprisingly good coffee at the Cowboy Cafe). Continue northeast on WY 120 across high desert to Cody — arriving mid-to-late afternoon.

Spend the rest of the day at the Buffalo Bill Center of the West if it's still open — five museums in one building (Buffalo Bill, Plains Indians, Western art, firearms, natural history). It closes at 5pm. If you arrive too late, save it for tomorrow morning before you push on.

Dinner at the Irma Hotel (Buffalo Bill's own hotel from 1902) — the prime rib buffet is famous and the cherry-wood backbar was a gift from Queen Victoria herself. Time it for the free Cody Gunfighters show at 6pm on the front porch. It's hokey, it's wonderful, you'll remember it.

Suggested Overnight
The Cody Hotel or Holiday Inn Cody at Buffalo Bill Village — both have elevators and easy parking. The Irma is historic and walkable to everything but has no elevator and stairs to the rooms. Dinner at the Irma, sleep elsewhere.
Day 8 · Friday, June 19
Cody → Sheridan, WY (Cloud Peak Skyway)
~210 mi · ~5 hr w/ stops

If you missed the Buffalo Bill Center of the West yesterday, do it this morning — plan 3 hours minimum. Then US 14A east out of Cody, over the Bighorn Mountains via Cloud Peak Skyway. This is another of the great American scenic drives — alpine meadows, hairpins, snow-capped peaks. The road tops out at almost 9,500 feet at Granite Pass.

Stop: the Medicine Wheel National Historic Landmark — a 75-foot-diameter stone wheel built by Native Americans 700+ years ago, sitting on top of a 9,600-foot mountain. There's a 1.5-mile flat walk in (no driving to the wheel itself, by design). It's one of the most sacred sites in the West and almost no cruisers ever see it.

Down the eastern slope to Sheridan — classic ranching town, well-preserved historic main street.

Suggested Overnight
The Sheridan Inn (1893, Buffalo Bill connections, recently renovated) or Holiday Inn Sheridan.
Day 9 · Saturday, June 20
Sheridan → Devils Tower → Rapid City, SD
~290 mi · ~5 hr w/ stops

I-90 east into the Black Hills country. Devils Tower is a 30-minute detour off I-90 at Sundance, WY — the country's first national monument, a 1,267-foot column of igneous rock that genuinely does not look real when you first see it. Walk the 1.3-mile flat path around its base and you'll see why everyone from Lakota holy men to Steven Spielberg has used this place as a touchstone.

Continue east through Sturgis on the way to Rapid City. Sturgis the town is small — you can drive Main Street in 3 minutes. Stop at the Sturgis Motorcycle Museum for an hour ($10 admission) and you've checked the box. The bikers don't arrive until August; mid-June Sturgis is a quiet small town.

Continue to Rapid City for the night. Walk the downtown after dinner — there are bronze sculptures of every U.S. president on the street corners (the "City of Presidents" project). It sounds gimmicky and it isn't.

Suggested Overnight
Hotel Alex Johnson (1928, historic, downtown, has elevator) or Hampton Inn Rapid City.
Day 10 · Sunday, June 21
Rapid City: Mt. Rushmore + Crazy Horse + Custer State Park
~120 mi loop

A full day in the Black Hills with Rapid City as your base. Start early — head to Mt. Rushmore for morning light on the faces (best photography is before 11 a.m.). Walk the Presidential Trail at the base. The Sculptor's Studio is small but excellent.

From Rushmore, drive the Iron Mountain Road south — one of the prettiest 17 miles in America. The road has three pigtail bridges and three tunnels carefully aligned to frame Mt. Rushmore in the distance as you drive through them. This is one to photograph.

Continue to Crazy Horse Memorial — still under construction (and will be for decades) but the visitor complex is rich and the carving is enormous. Then loop through Custer State Park on the way back — the Wildlife Loop Road has a famous resident bison herd and you'll likely see a hundred of them. Stay in your car if a bison is on the road; they'll move when they're ready.

Suggested Overnight
Same as last night — second night in Rapid City. Saves you from packing up.
Day 11 · Monday, June 22
Rapid City → Sioux Falls, SD (via Badlands & Wall Drug)
~360 mi · ~6 hr w/ stops

I-90 east, but with two important detours. First, Badlands National Park — turn south at exit 131 and drive the Badlands Loop Road (Highway 240) through the park. It's 32 miles and reconnects to I-90 at exit 110, so you're not really losing time. The landscape is a 75-million-year-old eroded paleontology site that looks like the surface of another planet. Don't skip this.

Second, Wall Drug at exit 110. You've seen the signs from 500 miles away. The pitch is "free ice water for travelers" — a tradition since 1936. The store itself is a delightful mess: a fake T-Rex, a chapel, a 1880s-style mall, a backyard, $0.05 coffee, donuts. Stop for an hour. Have lunch. It's ridiculous and it's also a real piece of American roadside history.
