What it's actually like onboard

The Grand Lobby — your first impression boarding Queen Elizabeth

The aesthetic is grand, traditional, and unapologetically British. The Grand Lobby pictured above gives you a sense of what to expect: hand-carved staircases, an Art Deco chandelier, marble floors. The Queens Room is the centerpiece of evenings, where afternoon tea is served daily and where most evenings end with proper ballroom dancing under chandeliers. The Royal Court Theatre handles the main shows. The Golden Lion pub serves draft beer and proper fish and chips. There's also a two-story library that reviewers regularly compare to Hogwarts.

Two formal nights per week are taken seriously — most men will be in suits or tuxedos, most women in cocktail dresses or long gowns. On non-formal "smart attire" nights, men still need a collared shirt and slacks at dinner. The dress code matters here in a way it doesn't on most other ships, and Cunard regulars appreciate the structure of it.

The crowd skews older and more reserved than on most cruise lines, but recent reviewers consistently note it's not actually stuffy — just polished and a bit quieter. Service is generally polished (Cunard calls it "White Star Service") with a few recent reviews noting it has slipped slightly. Food is good but not exceptional — strong British classics, weaker on more adventurous cuisine. Entertainment is refined rather than flashy.

What you'll likely love

  • Tracy Arm Fjord scenic day is a visual highlight of any Alaska cruise
  • Real afternoon tea, ballroom, and library — a proper traditional experience
  • Calmer, more curated atmosphere — feels like a ship for grown-ups
  • White Star service ethos: name recognition, attentive crew

What you should know
  • Dress code is enforced — packing a cocktail dress and dressier eveningwear is required

  • Entertainment is mellow — no big production shows or comedy
  • Lower cabin categories cannot dine in the Grills restaurants
  • Some recent reviews flag service slightly below the marketed standard
  • Mature crowd may feel quiet to those who want a livelier ship

What it's actually like onboard

The Poesia pool deck — refreshed in the April 2026 refurbishment

MSC is an Italian-headquartered, unabashedly European cruise line, and the onboard atmosphere reflects that. Decor leans Italian Mediterranean — brass-railed staircases, marble surfaces, an atrium waterfall, a starlit ceiling in the theater. Lounges have real character: the green-leather Hitchcock Lounge, the Zebra Bar, an Italian wine bar called Il Grappolo d'Oro. The April 2026 refurbishment refreshed cabins, public rooms, and added a new specialty dining lineup including the Butcher's Cut steakhouse and an expanded Kaito Sushi Bar.

The biggest cultural difference from Cunard or Holland America: announcements are made in five languages — English, Italian, Spanish, French, and German. Every announcement, every time. Reviewers consistently flag this; it takes some getting used to. Dinners tend to be longer and more leisurely (the Mediterranean way). Portion sizes are smaller than American norms, though waitstaff will gladly bring seconds. Entertainment leans toward acrobats, dance shows, and music rather than English-only comedy or production shows.

Service style is also different — friendly, but typically less proactive than American-tradition lines. Crew won't always anticipate every need the way a Cunard or HAL crew member might. The crowd skews younger than Cunard's (mostly 40s–60s) and more mixed in nationality. This is also MSC's first-ever Alaska season, which is a double-edged sword: the operation is still new (some logistical hiccups are likely), but new operations often try harder to impress.

Our Recommendation

Cunard Queen Elizabeth, Sept 17

For a once-in-a-while special trip — and for what amounts to $26 more per night than the MSC option — Queen Elizabeth gives you a more polished, more memorable, more "this was a real occasion" experience. The 2025 refurbishment is fresh enough that you won't feel the ship's age. Tracy Arm Fjord is one of the best scenic cruising days in Alaska. And the dress-up evenings, ballroom dancing, and afternoon tea have a way of turning an ordinary cruise into something you remember years later.

That said — if any of the following apply, MSC Poesia is genuinely the better choice for you:
  • You would dread packing formal attire
  • You'd prefer to keep the trip lower-key and use the savings on excursions or onboard extras
  • You've been to Alaska before and want a port itinerary that includes Icy Strait Point
  • You simply prefer a more relaxed European cruise feel over British tradition
The Poesia option is not a compromise — it's a legitimately different style of trip. Both ships are good fits for the right traveler.
